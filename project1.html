Matter

Matter is a cloud storage product focused on improving collaboration through extensive note and chat features. This project was part of a eight-month mentorship completed in 2019. Over a period of 4 weeks, I researched, designed, and determined the brand identity for my Bloc Designer Track project.

View prototype

PROBLEM

Given the versatile uses for cloud storage products, I found in my preliminary research (discussed in-depth below) that uses fit into three, very broad, categories: professional, school, and personal. What was common among each of these three that users were not just using these products for storage, but also to collaborate to meet deadlines and goals. With that, I further found that there was room for improvement in the collaboration tools within cloud storage products.

SOLUTION

A suite of features focused on increasing collaboration between users. Through comprehensive user research, I found that users were interested in incorporating chat and note sharing features, as well as a feature that would allow them to save content directly from the web. As such, Matter was designed with these user needs in mind.

RESEARCH

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

I first performed a competitive analysis of three major competitors (Dropbox, Google Drive, and Evernote), and identified an opportunity for a hybrid document management and note taking product, which offers the best of both types of products.

Through analysis of the common strengths of these products, I identified standard features that users are used to seeing in their current cloud storage product. I decided that priority was for these features to work seemlessly in Matter, and then to include the desired collaboration features identified within the user survey and interviews.

PERSONAS

Personas for this project represented a variety of roles, and were created based on interviews to understand their motivations, frustrations, and goals.

Further interviews with user participants to establish these personas brought another desirable feature to light-- the ability to chat within shared folders (in addition to shared documents). This would allow collaborators of shared folders to chat within them to reduce redundancy and miscommunication. Following these interviews, I identified an opportunity to improve collaboration, and decided to steer the product in that direction.

SKETCHES & LO-FI WIREFRAMES

Low-fidelity wireframes were created, tested, and underwent many different iterations before graduating to the the high-fidelity stage. The first round of testing took place in Invision, where users were asked to complete a set of tasks.

Overall, participants had positive responses to Matter and were able to navigate the site smoothly, with the one exception of one issue. All participants had a hard time adding a new file, as they all looked for this option on the homepage, but instead they had to first click into the “File” page. Since adding a new file is a high priority task, I fixed this problem by adding a “Create new file” option on the home page.

Brand & identity

Before jumping into the design and layout of the app, I established the overall look and feel of the brand.

Brainstorm

The users that were interviewed all had busy student and professional lives, all with the intention to slow down once business was handled. This brought me to the tagline “Focus on what matters”. From there, I performed a mind mapping exercise, which helped me narrow down to “Matter”.

Logo

Once the brand name was determined, I played around with different logo variations. First I sketched logos, playing with different “M” designs. From these sketches, I further developed three sketches into digital logos.

I performed preference testing over these three developed logos, resulting in the circular 'M' logo as the popular choice. I also preferred this logo, similar to the participants, for its clean and modern aesthetic. This logo was further developed to the final logo on the far right.

Color

I used a purple color as the brand color as it represents trust and creativity. Since I am including a feature where users can directly save web content to the application, one of the main uses will be to draw inspiration from the web. This brings an element of creativity into the experience.

I went with an analogous color scheme of blue, violet, and red based colors. The blue further emphasizes trust, while the red based colors offer a welcome complement to the purple used throughout the application.

Typography

For this project, the style choices made were done so in order to emulate brand trust, professionalism, and creativity. First and foremost, I knew I wanted sans-serif typefaces to create a modern look. I went with Oxygen as the brand typeface, and Source Sans Pro for the supplementary typeface. I found that these two typefaces had the right balance of professionalism and flare, without being outdated.

High fidelity mockups

I designed high fidelity mockups in Figma to bring Matter to life. First, I applied color and images to the layout, including custom illustrations for the landing page. For the iOS design, I studied and carefully applied Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines.

A/B Preference tests

Three A/B preference tests were performed in order to maintain user-centric design.

PREFERENCE TEST #1

More and share buttons

73% of participants preferred the option on the right, as it was more apparent that the “share” and “more” components were buttons. Furthermore, the outlined and colored buttons provided a consideration of hierarchy to the buttons. In order to improve usability, I used the colored and outlined buttons on the right.

PREFERENCE TEST #2

Landing page call-to-action buttons

73% of participants preferred the option on the right, as the pink buttons stood out better and brightened up the screen. In order to improve usability, I used the bright pink color on the right.

PREFERENCE TEST #3

Filter buttons

55% of participants preferred the option on the left, but the reasons were based on general preference. As the results were pretty even, and participant preference was not based on usability, I used the linear button on the left, to be consistent with the other buttons.

Usability tests

I performed usability tests over the high fidelity mockups in which participants were asked to complete high priority tasks such as create new content, organize content, and chat with collaborators. These same tasks were tested as part of the wireframe usability tests to develop the informational architecture.

High fidelity mockup - Second iteration

All participants of the prototype usability tests took longer to create a new note as opposed to creating any other content type. This told me that the “Create new note” feature should also be included on the home page, as note taking and sharing features are high priority tasks and important for collaboration.

Furthermore, one participant of the usability test had a hard time understanding the “note” content type. When she was asked to create a new note, she tried to select the “more” option on a document to add a comment. Since one other participant from the first wireframe usability test had the same issue, I decided to include this information on the landing page to introduce this content type.

Conclusion

One of the biggest lessons from this project was the importance of putting aside my ego and trusting the research. While designing the visual design, I lost confidence in my product, as I felt that it was not innovative enough. However, my research told me that users were looking for better collaboration features, and the users needs take priority over mine.

I learned the next lesson after the high-fidelity usability tests were performed, and realized that the issues identified were very similar to those identified in the wireframe usability tests. In the high-fidelity usability tests, participants had a hard time creating new notes, while in the wireframe usability tests, participants had a hard time creating new files. I realized that my questions in the first wireframe usability tests were not as specific as they could have been, and that this issue could have been identified much earlier had I asked the right questions earlier, but luckily the questions in the second round of testing were much more detailed and this issue was eventually identified.
